\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

\paragraph{LLM-as-a-Judge.}
Pairwise LLM judging is now a standard scalable evaluation paradigm~\citep{zheng2023judging}, and surveys document its position, length, and self-enhancement biases~\citep{li2024llmsasjudges}; mitigations include swap-and-compare and debate~\citep{wang2023faireval}, and prompt/rubric-template sensitivity is well established~\citep{wei2024systematic}. Two recent findings directly motivate our design: \citet{xu2026inside} give a mechanistic account showing judge biases arise from shared internal heuristics---so stability can be a warning signal but never a correctness guarantee, and verbalized confidence can be unfaithful---and \citet{li2025preference} document preference leakage, underscoring the need for strictly disjoint splits when calibrating any judge-reliability estimator.

\paragraph{Prompt-Sensitivity, Consistency, and Per-Instance Judge Reliability.}
A growing body of work studies how LLM outputs shift under semantically equivalent prompt, rubric, or ordering perturbations, and how such shifts bear on \emph{per-instance} reliability. \citet{hua2025flaw} ask whether paraphrase sensitivity is a flaw or an artifact; \citet{bryan2025order} isolate the input-order effect; \citet{anghel2025diagnosing} build a pairwise meta-evaluator diagnosing judge instability; and \citet{huang2026prompt} use prompt perturbation to stabilize pairwise evaluation. Closer to the per-instance regime, \citet{gupta2026diagnosing} diagnose judge reliability with conformal prediction sets, \citet{choi2026diagnosing} apply item-response theory, and \citet{li2025autoprompt} ensemble prompt variants to raise accuracy. \citet{ling2025abstention} caution that abstention can itself be a prompt artifact---a caveat we address via competence-gating (Section~\ref{sec:exp:stablewrong}).

We do \emph{not} claim to be the first to observe protocol sensitivity or to use a consistency signal; we differ in \emph{use} and \emph{scope}. In use, prior work diagnoses judges globally (\citealp{hua2025flaw,bryan2025order,anghel2025diagnosing}), builds per-instance reliability \emph{sets} for analysis (\citealp{gupta2026diagnosing,choi2026diagnosing}), or ensembles prompts to \emph{improve accuracy} (\citealp{li2025autoprompt,huang2026prompt}); we instead fuse three perturbation families into a single calibrated accept/abstain decision under an explicit risk budget. In scope, none characterizes the competence-gated asymmetry we report---instability predicts error above a competence threshold and inverts below it.

\paragraph{Selective Prediction and Abstention.}
Selective classification~\citep{geifman2017selective,el2010foundations} enables a predictor to abstain on uncertain inputs; \citet{wen2025know} survey abstention in LLMs (verbalized confidence, self-consistency, retrieval), \citet{krishnan2024enhancing} propose uncertainty-calibrated fine-tuning using hidden states, \citet{wang2025sconu} develop selective conformal uncertainty that screens high-uncertainty LLM queries with finite-sample error control, and \citet{tayebati2025learning} learn conformal abstention policies for adaptive risk management. Our work differs by focusing on \emph{evaluation-protocol stability} as the uncertainty source, which is specific to the judging task and does not require model internals or a learned abstention controller.

\paragraph{Risk-Controlled Selective Judging.}
Our work is closest to a recent line of risk-controlled selective LLM judging, and we are explicit that the statistical machinery is adopted, not invented. \citet{jung2025trust} (Trust-or-Escalate) proposed cascaded selective evaluation with human-agreement guarantees using Simulated Annotators plus Clopper--Pearson risk control; concurrently \citet{badshah2026scope} (SCOPE) aggregate both response orderings into a permutation-invariant conformal score, and \citet{sheng2025analyzing} apply conformal prediction to judge-uncertainty intervals. CARE-Judge reuses this apparatus (an exact Clopper--Pearson threshold on a held-out split) rather than proposing new guarantees. Our contribution is the \emph{confidence signal}: where Trust-or-Escalate relies on simulated-annotator agreement and SCOPE on bidirectional probability alone, we fuse rubric, position, and self-consistency instability into one calibrator and---crucially---characterize the capability regimes where such signals help or fail. SCOPE isolates permutation invariance while we treat position as one feature among several, so the two are complementary; CARE-Judge is a multi-view generalization rather than a competitor to any single signal.

\paragraph{Judge Benchmarks and Rubric-Based Evaluation.}
We use JudgeBench~\citep{tan2024judgebench} and RewardBench~\citep{lambert2024rewardbench} as hard, objectively-labeled benchmarks and TL;DR~\citep{stiennon2020learning} and LMArena~\citep{zheng2023judging} as subjective-preference benchmarks. Prior rubric work treats rubrics as fixed criteria to align with---\citet{hong2026from} align judges with human scoring rubrics and \citet{anghel2025pearl} propose a rubric-driven multi-metric framework---whereas we treat \emph{rubric sensitivity} (how much a verdict changes under semantically equivalent paraphrases) as an uncertainty signal. We are not aware of prior work that uses rubric-paraphrase instability specifically as a per-instance acceptance signal within a risk-controlled selective-judging framework; we frame this narrower claim as our contribution and validate it empirically.

\paragraph{Cost-Aware Cascades.}
Routing easy queries to cheap models and escalating hard ones is a well-established cost-reduction strategy; \citet{zellinger2025costsaving} study LLM cascades with early abstention and formal cost analysis. Our exploratory cascade experiment (Appendix) applies the same idea to judge routing and defers a formal cascade-level guarantee to future work.
