\begin{abstract}
Large language models are widely used as automated judges, yet they are overconfident and sensitive to how an evaluation protocol is worded. We study whether a judge's \emph{protocol (in)stability} is a reliable per-instance error signal. A judge is protocol-stable on an item if its verdict survives equivalent rubric paraphrases, response-order swaps, and resampling. We find that instability signals error, but only under a condition we call \textbf{competence-gating}. Above a judge-accuracy threshold, stable verdicts are up to 27 points more accurate than unstable ones. Below it the relationship reverses, and a near-random judge is confidently and consistently wrong on the majority of its errors, so stability no longer implies correctness. A negative control and an error decomposition rule out that this reversal is an artifact of wording noise.

We build \textbf{CARE-Judge} (Calibrated, Abstaining, and Robust Evaluation) on this finding. It fuses the three stability families into a learned correctness calibrator, then accepts or abstains under an explicit risk budget set by an exact Clopper--Pearson threshold. We test five judges, spanning a controlled Qwen2.5 size ladder and two API models, on four benchmarks. Fusion improves the risk--coverage tradeoff most in the mid-accuracy band, matches an already saturated frontier judge, and abstains when a judge is near chance. This is the pattern competence-gating predicts. CARE-Judge therefore turns an unreliable judge into a selective evaluator that knows when to withhold a verdict, and it maps out precisely when protocol stability can be trusted as a reliability signal.
\end{abstract}
