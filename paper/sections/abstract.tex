\begin{abstract}
Large language models are increasingly used as automated judges to evaluate generated text, yet these judges are prone to overconfidence, positional bias, and sensitivity to evaluation-protocol wording.
We propose \textbf{CARE-Judge} (Calibrated, Abstaining, and Robust Evaluation), a selective evaluation framework that determines \emph{when} an LLM judge's verdict should be trusted.
Our insight is \emph{protocol-stability} used as an asymmetric signal: when a judge's verdict changes under semantically equivalent evaluation protocols---rubric paraphrases, response-order permutations, and stochastic resampling---the verdict is often unreliable, whereas stability alone is \emph{not} evidence of correctness, since a judge can be stably wrong.
CARE-Judge fuses these protocol-stability features into a learned correctness calibrator and reuses established selective-risk machinery (fixed-sequence testing with an exact Clopper--Pearson bound) to accept verdicts at a user-specified risk level; we adopt this machinery from prior work rather than claiming it as a contribution.
Across three judge models (Qwen2.5-1.5B, DeepSeek-V4, GPT-5.5) and three benchmarks (JudgeBench, TL;DR, MT-Bench Human), protocol-stability signals separate correct from incorrect verdicts by 12--54 percentage points---but only above a minimum judge competence.
At a matched six-call budget the fused calibrator attains the best AUROC in seven of nine judge--dataset pairs, beating base confidence, self-consistency, rubric, and the SCOPE-style bidirectional signal; on JudgeBench with GPT-5.5 it accepts 99.6\% of items at 91.5\% accuracy under a 15\% risk budget, and 43\% at 99\% accuracy under a 5\% budget.
Critically, when the judge is near-random (Qwen-1.5B on JudgeBench, 49.4\%) its verdicts are stable-but-wrong and the signals reverse, so CARE-Judge abstains entirely---a boundary condition we treat as a finding rather than a success.
A cost-aware cascade with conditional per-tier calibration and a union-bound risk budget routes most TL;DR items to a free local judge (74--98\% API-token savings), and calibrators transfer across datasets for strong judges.
\end{abstract}
