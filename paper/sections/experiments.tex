\section{Experiments}
\label{sec:experiments}

\subsection{Experimental Setup}
\label{sec:exp:setup}

\paragraph{Datasets.}
We use four benchmarks spanning objective correctness and subjective preference: JudgeBench~\citep{tan2024judgebench} (620 objectively-labeled pairs over knowledge/reasoning/math/coding), RewardBench~\citep{lambert2024rewardbench} (chosen/rejected pairs over chat/safety/reasoning), TL;DR~\citep{stiennon2020learning} (summarization preference), and LMArena~\citep{zheng2023judging} (Chatbot-Arena human preference). We scale each subjective benchmark to 2{,}000 pairs (dropping ties) so the 40/30/30 split gives calibration/test folds of several hundred items---enough for the exact Clopper--Pearson bound to certify nonzero coverage. We redistribute only derived feature traces, not raw text, under each benchmark's license.

\paragraph{Judge Models.}
We use five judges. Throughout, ``below-threshold'', ``mid-accuracy'', and ``frontier'' refer to a judge's \emph{raw agreement on the benchmark at hand} (near chance, $0.65$--$0.80$, and above, respectively), not to the underlying model's general capability; the same model can fall in different bands on different benchmarks (Section~\ref{sec:exp:modelsize}). Two API judges cover the mid-to-frontier range as judges: \textbf{DeepSeek-V4} in non-thinking \texttt{deepseek-chat} mode (its judge agreement lands in the mid band; the reasoning \texttt{v4-pro} mode is too token-intensive for six-call collection) and \textbf{GPT-5.5} (frontier agreement, default reasoning effort). The other three form a \emph{controlled} same-family ladder---\textbf{Qwen2.5-1.5B/7B/14B-Instruct}---holding recipe, tokenizer, and prompt fixed so that only parameter count varies, isolating a size-driven trend in judge agreement from the broader (confounded) API-vs-open comparison. Base confidence is verbalized confidence, since log-probabilities are not universally exposed. Exact snapshot identifiers, dates, decoding settings, and invalid-output handling are in Appendix~\ref{app:repro}. (GLM-5.2 was attempted but behaves as a slow reasoning model on the available endpoint, so we omit it.)

\paragraph{Feature Collection, Protocol, and Baselines.}
Each item uses six judge calls: one base ($\tau{=}0$), two self-consistency samples ($\tau{=}0.7$, pooled to $S{=}3$), one position swap, and two rubric paraphrases ($K{=}3$ with the base rubric). Unparseable model outputs are retried once, then recorded as an \texttt{invalid} state that counts as a disagreement (lowering, never inflating, stability); genuine parse-invalid rates are $<3\%$ everywhere. Separately, a subset of GPT-5.5 API calls failed with transient service errors (rate-limit/quota) rather than model outputs; these are not judge behavior, so the affected items are excluded from GPT-5.5's analysis and we report its per-benchmark retained $N$ in Table~\ref{tab:main} (Appendix~\ref{app:repro}). Ties are dropped on the preference benchmarks. All results use a strict 40/30/30 train/calibration/test split (fit calibrator / select threshold / report), 20 seeds, $\alpha{=}0.15$, $\delta{=}0.10$, exact Clopper--Pearson. At the matched six-call budget we compare \emph{Raw}, \emph{CARE} (logistic fusion), the single signals \emph{base}/\emph{rubric}/\emph{swap} (a budget-matched reimplementation of SCOPE's bidirectional signal~\citep{badshah2026scope})/\emph{self}, oracle \emph{best-single}, and a budget-matched Trust-or-Escalate simulated-annotator baseline~\citep{jung2025trust}.

\paragraph{Protocol instability and judge error (RQ1).}
Across all four primary judges, rubric- and position-stable verdicts are more accurate than unstable ones on almost every benchmark (Fig.~\ref{fig:gap}, Appendix~\ref{app:signals}, recomputed from the per-item feature traces). For DeepSeek-V4 the rubric gap ranges from $+4.1$pp (JudgeBench) to $+27.4$pp (RewardBench, $0.937$ vs.\ $0.664$). The gap persists for the frontier GPT-5.5 (e.g.\ $+27.0$pp rubric on RewardBench) but its unstable subset is small (rubric-flip $<$8\% of items), so the signal flags few verdicts and bounds how much it can add once a judge is near-saturated (RQ2). The one systematic reversal is the below-threshold Qwen2.5-1.5B on RewardBench, where rubric- and position-stable verdicts are \emph{less} accurate ($-10.9$ and $-11.0$pp): a near-random judge is stably wrong, the mechanism we examine next.

\emph{Negative control.} To rule out that equivalent-rubric flips are mere wording noise, we contrast the $K{=}3$ equivalent paraphrases against an \emph{intentionally non-equivalent} rubric set (brevity/thoroughness/formality) on JudgeBench. On DeepSeek-V4 the non-equivalent set flips $2.2\times$ more often ($28.0\%$ vs.\ $12.8\%$), so equivalent-rubric flips are a conservative lower bound on real instability, not artifacts; the near-random Qwen-1.5B shows no such ordering ($31.0\%$ vs.\ $16.7\%$), being insensitive to what the rubric asks.

\paragraph{Where fusion helps (RQ2).}
RQ2 asks not whether fusion helps \emph{on average} across all judges---RQ1 predicts it should not---but whether it helps in the mid-accuracy band where the signal is informative. Two independent judges occupy that band: DeepSeek-V4 (a strong general model whose \emph{judge} agreement nonetheless lands in the mid band) and Qwen2.5-7B (an open ladder model), which share no training recipe. On DeepSeek-V4, fusion improves over the best single signal by $+6.3$ AUROC on LMArena ($0.615\to0.678$), $+5.9$ on TL;DR ($0.616\to0.675$), and $+4.4$ on RewardBench ($0.787\to0.831$), with paired-bootstrap 95\% CIs over the 20 seeds excluding zero on those three benchmarks (JudgeBench, at $+1.0$, includes zero). On Qwen2.5-7B fusion is positive on three of the four benchmarks ($+7.6$/$+2.0$/$+1.1$ AUROC on RB/TL;DR/LMArena over the best single signal) but falls $3.0$ below the swap signal on the objective-label JudgeBench, where a 7B open judge is near chance. Seven of the eight mid-accuracy pairs thus show positive fusion gains, as competence-gating predicts.

On the frontier GPT-5.5 fusion ties its already-strong base confidence to within noise (e.g.\ $0.834$ vs.\ $0.845$ on JudgeBench and $0.888$ vs.\ $0.904$ on RewardBench, where base confidence edges fusion by $\leq0.02$), consistent with there being little instability left to exploit; below the competence threshold no signal exceeds $\approx0.58$ on Qwen-1.5B's subjective benchmarks.

The regime boundary is fixed \emph{before} any fusion result is examined: RQ1 shows instability tracks error only for judges of at least moderate raw accuracy, so we assign each judge to a regime by its raw agreement alone---mid-accuracy at $0.65$--$0.80$ (Qwen2.5-7B, DeepSeek-V4), frontier above it (GPT-5.5), below-floor near chance (Qwen2.5-1.5B). The pre-specified primary test is therefore restricted to the eight mid-accuracy pairs: fusion improves seven of them ($+1.0$ to $+7.6$ AUROC), the sole exception being Qwen2.5-7B on JudgeBench. As a \emph{secondary} comparison, pooling all 17 judge--benchmark pairs---including regimes where fusion cannot help by construction---gives a one-sided Wilcoxon $p{=}0.087$ (11/17 wins); the direction of the test follows RQ1's directional prediction that fusion should not hurt. The gap between the mid-accuracy result and the marginal pooled one is what competence-gating predicts: pooling averages in the frontier and below-threshold regimes where no signal can help. In selective terms, at $\alpha{=}0.15$ CARE raises accepted accuracy above raw by abstaining on the risky tail (e.g.\ DeepSeek-V4/TL;DR: 8.5\% coverage at 82.9\% accuracy, raw 67.9\%), while a saturated judge already meeting the budget is accepted almost entirely (GPT-5.5/RewardBench 99.7\% at 96.1\%); the ``Viol.'' column reports risk-control violations, expected occasionally at low coverage under the calibration-conditional target (Appendix~\ref{app:rcfig}).

\paragraph{The competence boundary (RQ3).}
Below a minimum judge capability the signal inverts: where Qwen2.5-1.5B is near chance (JudgeBench $0.49$, TL;DR/LMArena $0.58$) its stable verdicts are not more correct, so CARE-Judge accepts zero examples rather than trust them. RewardBench is the exception: there the same 1.5B judge is more accurate (raw $0.77$) and fusion behaves as in the mid-accuracy regime ($0.713$ AUROC, $51.9\%$ coverage at $88.0\%$). Competence is therefore per task rather than per model---the threshold tracks how accurate the judge is on the data at hand. We quantify the near-chance boundary in Section~\ref{sec:exp:stablewrong}; safety (abstention) and usefulness (coverage) must be judged jointly.

\subsection{Head-to-Head System Comparison}
\label{sec:exp:comparison}

We compare CARE directly against the two prior selective-judging \emph{systems}---SCOPE and Trust-or-Escalate---at the same six-call budget (full 17-pair table in Appendix~\ref{app:threeway}). CARE attains the highest AUROC on 14 of the 17 pairs, including all eight DeepSeek/GPT pairs; the three exceptions are Qwen judges on JudgeBench, where SCOPE's bidirectional score is marginally higher near the accuracy floor. The margin over SCOPE's bidirectional-consistency score is large for DeepSeek-V4 (0.831 vs.\ 0.606 on RewardBench) and remains clear for GPT-5.5 (0.888 vs.\ 0.757 on RewardBench): a frontier judge rarely flips under reordering, so the consistency signal is only weakly informative, whereas fusion additionally leverages its well-calibrated base confidence. Trust-or-Escalate's agreement hovers near chance ($0.50$--$0.53$) at $N{=}0$ dedicated annotators. No single signal is uniformly best; the learned fusion is what delivers robustness across the judge-accuracy spectrum.

\subsection{Why Stability Is Not Correctness}
\label{sec:exp:stablewrong}

We decompose Qwen-1.5B's 620 JudgeBench verdicts by correctness and rubric stability: 65\% of its 314 incorrect verdicts are \emph{rubric-stable-wrong} (203/314), and only 51.1\% of its 415 rubric-stable verdicts are correct---essentially chance. This quantifies the competence boundary underpinning RQ3 and is consistent with mechanistic analyses of judge bias~\citep{xu2026inside,panickssery2024evaluators}: a shared heuristic yields highly consistent but wrong answers.

\emph{What the stable-but-wrong errors look like.} These 203 items reflect a confidently held wrong heuristic, not noise: their mean verbalized confidence ($0.797$) is indistinguishable from that on \emph{correct} verdicts ($0.785$), so confidence is useless exactly where a stability signal is meant to help---and it still fails. Such a gap between \emph{stated} and \emph{revealed} preference is well documented in choice modeling~\citep{yu2018quantum}, motivating our use of behavior under perturbation rather than self-reported confidence. Nor are they a length artifact: on these errors the judge picks the longer response $55.2\%$ of the time (vs.\ $51.6\%$ overall), so the shared heuristic is semantic, not a crude length preference. Below the floor the protocol signals do not merely weaken---they cease to co-vary with correctness, the operational content of ``competence-gating.''

\subsection{Ensemble Baselines and Feature-Group Ablation}
\label{sec:exp:ablation}

Against two ensemble baselines (majority/weighted vote) and a by-group feature ablation (full tables in Appendix~\ref{app:ensemble}), CARE is best or tied-best in 12 of 16 pairs. Weighted vote is competitive for GPT-5.5 (it uses confidence implicitly) but has no abstention mechanism: a vote must rule on every item, whereas on DeepSeek-V4/TL;DR CARE abstains to 8.5\% coverage at 82.9\% accuracy versus the raw 67.9\%. The group ablation confirms complementary roles: confidence dominates for GPT-5.5, protocol features carry the ladder judges, and length is near-chance throughout.

\subsection{Judge-Capability Dependence: A Controlled Ladder}
\label{sec:exp:modelsize}

Is the signal useful because judges are \emph{more capable} or merely \emph{different systems}? A \emph{controlled} same-family ladder (Qwen2.5-1.5B/7B/14B) on JudgeBench varies only parameter count (Table~\ref{tab:ladder}): raw accuracy rises monotonically with scale ($0.49\to0.57\to0.60$), while fused AUROC rises from 1.5B to 7B ($.52{\pm}.06\to.55{\pm}.04$) and then does not increase at 14B ($.54{\pm}.04$). Since the 7B and 14B values differ by less than a seed-level standard deviation, we read the signal as \emph{saturating by the 7B scale}, consistent with gains concentrated in the mid-accuracy transition rather than growing linearly with size. We restrict the ladder to JudgeBench's 620 objective-labeled items (sufficient to isolate the size effect at fixed recipe; the 14B judge runs at only $\approx200$ items/hour). The cross-system comparison agrees---on JudgeBench DeepSeek-V4 (raw 0.68) yields AUROC 0.60 and GPT-5.5 (raw 0.91) yields 0.83---but as the API judges differ in recipe we call that gap capability-suggestive, not controlled.

\paragraph{The mid-accuracy replication (Qwen2.5-7B).}
The 7B rung doubles as the second mid-accuracy judge underpinning RQ2 (Section~\ref{sec:exp:main}): sharing DeepSeek-V4's accuracy band but none of its recipe, it makes the fusion claim a recipe-independent replication, positive on three of its four benchmarks (the exception being the objective-label JudgeBench), reproducible via the released \texttt{scripts/run\_e1\_midcap.sh}.

\subsection{Robustness and Calibration}
\label{sec:exp:robustness}

Over 20 random splits on DeepSeek/TL;DR, fused AUROC is $0.675 \pm 0.016$ (CV 2.4\%); GPT-5.5's fused scores are well calibrated (ECE $\leq 0.06$) while DeepSeek-V4 is looser (ECE 0.16--0.29). Sweeping $\delta \in \{0.05, 0.10, 0.20\}$ and calibration-set size ($n_{\text{cal}} \in [200, 1000]$) leaves AUROC essentially unchanged ($0.676 \pm 0.001$), so CARE-Judge does not require a large labeled calibration set. Varying the risk budget $\alpha$ from $0.05$ to $0.30$ traces the expected coverage/accuracy tradeoff for the two API judges (Table~\ref{tab:alpha}), and the corresponding held-out risk--coverage curves are shown in Appendix~\ref{app:rcfig} (Figure~\ref{fig:rc}).
