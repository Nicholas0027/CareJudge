\section{Experiments}
\label{sec:experiments}

\subsection{Experimental Setup}
\label{sec:exp:setup}

\paragraph{Datasets.}
We evaluate on three benchmarks spanning objective correctness and subjective preference:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{JudgeBench}~\citep{tan2024judgebench}: 620 pairwise comparisons with objective correctness labels across knowledge, reasoning, math, and coding.
    \item \textbf{TL;DR}~\citep{stiennon2020learning}: 300 pairwise summarization preference comparisons from the OpenAI summarization feedback dataset.
    \item \textbf{MT-Bench Human}~\citep{zheng2023judging}: 300 pairwise human preference judgments of open-ended assistant responses.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Judge Models.}
We use three models spanning a wide capability range: \textbf{Qwen2.5-1.5B-Instruct} (a small local open-source model, 1.5B parameters), \textbf{DeepSeek-V4} in non-thinking chat mode (a mid-capability API model), and \textbf{GPT-5.5} (a frontier API model). We stress that these differ simultaneously in size, training recipe, and API configuration, so we interpret cross-model trends as \emph{judge-capability} effects rather than controlled model-size effects (Section~\ref{sec:exp:modelsize}). Exact model identifiers, version dates, decoding temperatures, and invalid-output handling are given in the Appendix.

\paragraph{Feature Collection.}
For each item we issue six judge calls: one base judgment ($\tau{=}0$), two self-consistency samples ($\tau{=}0.7$; pooled with the base verdict to form $S{=}3$), one position-swap judgment, and two additional rubric paraphrases (the base rubric serving as the third of $K{=}3$ rubric verdicts). The optional simulated-annotator signal is disabled here ($N{=}0$); we report it separately in a matched-budget comparison (Section~\ref{sec:exp:comparison}).

\paragraph{Evaluation Protocol.}
The cascade and cross-dataset transfer analyses (Appendices~\ref{app:cascade}--\ref{app:transfer}) now use the same strict 40/30/30 split, conditional per-tier calibration, and a union-bound error budget (Section~\ref{sec:method:cascade}).
ref{sec:method:cascade}).

\paragraph{Baselines and matched budget.}
We compare \emph{Raw} (accept all, no abstention); \emph{Logistic fusion} (CARE-Judge, all protocol-stability features); \emph{Best single signal} (highest-AUROC signal on train); \emph{Max-signal} (maximum of rubric, swap, and self vote shares); and three single-signal ablations \emph{Rubric-only}, \emph{Swap-only}, \emph{Self-only}. All of these consume the \emph{same} six-call budget, so their comparison is budget-matched. We additionally note that a single-call \emph{base-confidence} baseline and the simulated-annotator-only baseline of \citet{jung2025trust} use fewer or different calls; a fully budget-matched head-to-head against Trust-or-Escalate and SCOPE~\citep{badshah2026scope} is an important comparison that we were unable to complete under the current compute window and mark as primary future work.

\paragraph{Finding 1 (RQ1): Protocol \emph{instability} predicts judge error in competent regimes.}
Across all model--dataset pairs where the judge is at least moderately accurate ($\geq 60\%$), rubric perturbation and position-swap inconsistency mark substantially lower-accuracy subsets (Table~\ref{tab:signals}).
The largest gap is position-swap consistency on TL;DR with DeepSeek-V4: 78.3\% accuracy when consistent vs.\ 24.3\% when inconsistent (54.0pp); for GPT-5.5 on TL;DR, rubric stability yields a 43.0pp gap (77.5\% vs.\ 34.5\%).
Crucially, the relationship is \emph{asymmetric and competence-gated}: on Qwen-1.5B/JudgeBench, where the judge is near chance (49.4\%), the rubric gap reverses to $-8.9$pp---stable verdicts are no more accurate than unstable ones (Figure~\ref{fig:gap}). We therefore read these signals as \emph{instability$\Rightarrow$suspect} rather than \emph{stability$\Rightarrow$correct}, and restrict Table~\ref{tab:signals} to the regime where the direction holds.

\paragraph{Finding 2 (RQ2): Fusion improves the risk--coverage tradeoff at a matched budget.}
At the matched six-call budget, the fused calibrator (logistic or gradient-boosted) attains the best or tied-best AUROC in 7 of 9 model--dataset pairs, beating every single-signal baseline (Table~\ref{tab:budget}); the two exceptions are the near-random Qwen/JudgeBench regime and the hard GPT-5.5/MT-Bench pair, where no signal is informative. In selective terms, at $\alpha{=}0.15$ CARE-Judge lifts pooled accepted accuracy well above the raw baseline: from 65.2\% to 79.5\% on DeepSeek/TL;DR, 74.6\% to 83.5\% on GPT-5.5/TL;DR, and 67.4\% to 88.3\% on DeepSeek/JudgeBench, while on GPT-5.5/JudgeBench it accepts 99.6\% of items at 91.5\% accuracy. Figure~\ref{fig:rc} shows the full risk--coverage curves; the pooled sweep (Table~\ref{tab:alpha}) is monotone in $\alpha$ up to small residual floor effects. Crucially we report the realized risk-violation frequency $\Pr_{\text{seed}}(\hat R_{\text{test}}>\alpha)$ per setting (Appendix~\ref{app:repro}); because the $(1{-}\delta)$ guarantee concerns the calibration draw, occasional test violations at low coverage (where 90-item test splits accept only a handful of items) are expected and do not contradict the bound.

\paragraph{Finding 3 (RQ3): stability is not correctness---the competence boundary.}
When Qwen2.5-1.5B (49.4\% on JudgeBench) judges, 98\% of its incorrect verdicts are \emph{stable-wrong}: the judge confidently produces the same wrong answer across all three rubric variants, with near-perfect agreement (Section~\ref{sec:exp:stablewrong}). The rubric gap reverses ($-8.9$pp) and no threshold meets the risk budget, so CARE-Judge accepts zero examples under any $\alpha \leq 0.30$.
This is a \emph{competence boundary}, not a failure: below a minimum judge capability, a shared erroneous heuristic makes verdicts consistent without making them correct, exactly the failure mode identified mechanistically by \citet{xu2026inside}. Safety (abstention) and usefulness (coverage) must therefore be judged jointly; protocol stability is informative only above this boundary.

\subsection{Head-to-Head System Comparison}
\label{sec:exp:comparison}

Table~\ref{tab:threeway} compares CARE against both prior systems at the same six-call budget.
No method dominates.
On objective tasks at the frontier (GPT-5.5/JudgeBench), CARE's multi-signal fusion delivers the strongest ranking (AUROC 0.832 vs.\ 0.792 for SCOPE) with near-perfect coverage (99.6\%); on subjective tasks at mid-capability (DeepSeek/TL;DR, DeepSeek/MT-Bench), SCOPE's bidirectional-consistency signal alone achieves better AUROC.
T-o-E simulated agreement is the most conservative, generating usable coverage only when the judge is already excellent (GPT-5.5/JudgeBench: 94.3\% coverage at 92.6\% accuracy).
These regimes \emph{are precisely the pattern our hypothesis predicts}: when the judge is strong and the task is objective, multiple complementary stability signals (rubric, swap, self) each contribute independent information, and fusion extracts a gain over any one; when the judge is weaker or the task is subjective, the strongest single signal already saturates, and fusion cannot improve.
This regime-dependent complementarity---rather than a universal win---is the paper's central empirical message.

\subsection{Why Stability Is Not Correctness}
\label{sec:exp:stablewrong}

We decompose Qwen-1.5B's 620 verdicts on JudgeBench by correctness and signal agreement (agreement ${\ge}\,0.8$ on all three signals). Of 312 incorrect verdicts, 306 (98\%) are \emph{stable-wrong}: the judge outputs the same wrong answer consistently across rubrics, making the signal non-discriminative. Of 607 stable verdicts, only 301 are correct (49.6\%)---essentially chance. This quantifies the boundary condition underpinning RQ3 and is consistent with recent mechanistic analyses of judge bias~\citep{xu2026inside}: a shared heuristic (e.g., length or fluency) yields highly consistent but wrong answers, producing deceptively high protocol stability.

\subsection{Judge-Capability Dependence}
\label{sec:exp:modelsize}

Comparing the three judges confirms that protocol-stability signals become informative only above a capability threshold. On JudgeBench, Qwen-1.5B (49.4\%) produces reversed signal gaps and near-zero coverage, DeepSeek-V4 (67.4\%) produces consistent 12--16pp gaps, and GPT-5.5 (91.3\%) produces 12--13pp gaps with fused AUROC 0.832. We deliberately call this \emph{judge-capability} dependence rather than model-size dependence: the three judges differ in size, training recipe, and API configuration simultaneously, so we cannot attribute the trend to parameter count alone. Establishing a clean capability ladder with several controlled mid-size open models is important future work; the present three-point comparison should be read as suggestive of a competence threshold, not as an isolated parameter-count effect.
