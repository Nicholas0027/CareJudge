\section{Experiments}
\label{sec:experiments}

\subsection{Experimental Setup}
\label{sec:exp:setup}

\paragraph{Datasets.}
We use four benchmarks spanning objective correctness and subjective preference: JudgeBench~\citep{tan2024judgebench} (620 objectively-labeled pairs over knowledge/reasoning/math/coding), RewardBench~\citep{lambert2024rewardbench} (chosen/rejected pairs over chat/safety/reasoning), TL;DR~\citep{stiennon2020learning} (summarization preference), and LMArena~\citep{zheng2023judging} (Chatbot-Arena human preference). We scale each subjective benchmark to 2{,}000 pairs (dropping ties) so the 40/30/30 split gives calibration/test folds of several hundred items---enough for the exact Clopper--Pearson bound to certify nonzero coverage. We redistribute only derived feature traces, not raw text, under each benchmark's license.

\paragraph{Judge Models.}
We use five judges. Two API judges span the mid-to-frontier range: \textbf{DeepSeek-V4} in non-thinking \texttt{deepseek-chat} mode (mid-capability; the reasoning \texttt{v4-pro} mode is too token-intensive for six-call collection) and \textbf{GPT-5.5} (frontier, default reasoning effort). The other three form a \emph{controlled} same-family ladder---\textbf{Qwen2.5-1.5B/7B/14B-Instruct}---holding recipe, tokenizer, and prompt fixed so that only parameter count varies, isolating a size-driven capability trend from the broader (confounded) API-vs-open comparison. Base confidence is verbalized confidence, since log-probabilities are not universally exposed. Exact snapshot identifiers, dates, decoding settings, and invalid-output handling are in Appendix~\ref{app:repro}. (GLM-5.2 was attempted but behaves as a slow reasoning model on the available endpoint, so we omit it.)

\paragraph{Feature Collection, Protocol, and Baselines.}
Each item uses six judge calls: one base ($\tau{=}0$), two self-consistency samples ($\tau{=}0.7$, pooled to $S{=}3$), one position swap, and two rubric paraphrases ($K{=}3$ with the base rubric). Unparseable outputs are retried once, then recorded as an \texttt{invalid} state that counts as a disagreement (lowering, never inflating, stability); invalid rates are $<3\%$ everywhere (Appendix~\ref{app:repro}). Ties are dropped on the preference benchmarks. All results use a strict 40/30/30 train/calibration/test split (fit calibrator / select threshold / report), 20 seeds, $\alpha{=}0.15$, $\delta{=}0.10$, exact Clopper--Pearson. At the matched six-call budget we compare \emph{Raw}, \emph{CARE} (logistic fusion), the single signals \emph{base}/\emph{rubric}/\emph{swap} (a budget-matched reimplementation of SCOPE's bidirectional signal~\citep{badshah2026scope})/\emph{self}, oracle \emph{best-single}, and a budget-matched Trust-or-Escalate simulated-annotator baseline~\citep{jung2025trust}.

\paragraph{Finding 1 (RQ1): protocol \emph{instability} predicts error for mid-capability judges.}
For DeepSeek-V4 (verdicts flip on 6--20\% of items), rubric and position-swap instability cleanly separate lower-accuracy subsets on all four benchmarks (Fig.~\ref{fig:gap}, Appendix~\ref{app:signals}): the RewardBench gap is largest, with rubric-stable verdicts at 93.7\% accuracy versus 66.4\% when unstable ($+27.4$pp), and all DeepSeek gaps are positive ($+4.1$ to $+27.4$pp). The relationship is \emph{capability-gated and can invert}: for the frontier GPT-5.5 (rubric-flip $<$8\%) unstable items are not systematically wrong, and on RewardBench the gap even reverses ($-5.3$pp rubric, $-14.2$pp position) as its rare disagreements fall on genuinely ambiguous pairs. The signal is thus a mid-capability warning, not a universal law; the learned calibrator (Finding~2) still recovers usable structure where a single raw gap is small or reversed.

\emph{Negative control: instability tracks genuine sensitivity, not paraphrase noise.} To rule out that equivalent-rubric flips are mere wording noise, we contrast our $K{=}3$ equivalent paraphrases against an \emph{intentionally non-equivalent} rubric set (brevity/thoroughness/formality) on JudgeBench. On DeepSeek-V4 the non-equivalent set flips $2.2\times$ more often ($28.0\%$ vs.\ $12.8\%$): equivalent-rubric flips are thus a conservative lower bound on real instability, not artifacts. The same control on the near-random Qwen-1.5B shows no such ordering ($31.0\%$ vs.\ $16.7\%$, if anything reversed), as a below-floor judge is insensitive to what the rubric asks---closing the loop on the asymmetry.

\paragraph{Finding 2 (RQ2): fusion helps precisely in the competence regime the mechanism predicts.}
The correct test of RQ2 is not whether fusion helps \emph{on average} across all judges---Finding~1 predicts it should not---but whether it helps \emph{wherever the signal is informative}, i.e.\ in the mid-capability band. We test this on the \emph{two} independent judges that occupy that band, DeepSeek-V4 (a frontier-lab API model) and Qwen2.5-7B (an open ladder model), which share no training recipe and thus provide a genuine replication rather than a single anecdote. On DeepSeek-V4, fusion improves over the best single signal by $+6.0$ AUROC on TL;DR ($0.616\to0.675$), $+6.3$ on LMArena ($0.615\to0.678$), and $+4.4$ on RewardBench ($0.787\to0.831$), with paired-bootstrap 95\% CIs over the 20 seeds excluding zero on three of four benchmarks (TL;DR $[+0.05,+0.07]$, RB $[+0.02,+0.07]$, LMArena $[+0.05,+0.07]$; JudgeBench includes zero). On Qwen2.5-7B the same effect replicates where measured: fusion gives $+1.7$ AUROC on JudgeBench ($0.531\to0.548$) and $+4.2$ on RewardBench ($0.601\to0.643$), the RewardBench CI excluding zero ($[+0.02,+0.06]$); the two subjective points are the in-progress E1 collection (Section~\ref{sec:exp:modelsize}), and the competence-gating account makes the falsifiable prediction that they too will be positive. That the improvement appears on two recipe-independent mid-capability judges---not one---is the core evidence for RQ2.

By contrast, on the frontier GPT-5.5 fusion ties near-perfect base confidence to within noise ($0.986$ vs.\ $0.988$ on JudgeBench, the one pair a raw signal edges fusion), as it must when no instability remains to exploit; and below the competence floor no signal exceeds $\approx0.59$ on Qwen-1.5B's subjective benchmarks. Aggregating across \emph{all} regimes therefore dilutes the real effect with settings where fusion cannot help by construction: over 15 pairs a one-sided Wilcoxon test gives $p{=}0.13$, and over the 11 non-saturated pairs $p{=}0.09$; restricting to the mid-capability judges where the mechanism operates, fusion wins every measured pair. We read the non-significant \emph{global} average as a confirmation of competence-gating rather than a weakness---a method that improved a saturated frontier judge would contradict the mechanism. In selective terms, at $\alpha{=}0.15$ CARE lifts accepted accuracy well above raw (e.g.\ GPT-5.5/RewardBench: 81.9\% coverage at 88.1\% accuracy, raw 71.0\%); the ``Viol.'' column is our direct check on risk control, with occasional low-coverage violations expected under the calibration-conditional target (Appendix~\ref{app:rcfig}).

\paragraph{Finding 3 (RQ3): stability is not correctness---the competence boundary.}
Below a minimum judge capability the signal inverts: a near-random judge produces \emph{stable-but-wrong} verdicts, so CARE-Judge correctly accepts zero examples on JudgeBench/TL;DR/LMArena for Qwen2.5-1.5B and only a small fraction on RewardBench where it is more accurate. We quantify this boundary in Section~\ref{sec:exp:stablewrong}; the practical consequence is that safety (abstention) and usefulness (coverage) must be judged jointly.

The margin over SCOPE's bidirectional signal is modest for DeepSeek-V4 (0.831 vs.\ 0.606 on RewardBench) but dramatic for GPT-5.5, where the bare swap signal is \emph{actively misleading} (AUROC $<0.33$): a frontier judge almost never flips under reordering, so swap is uninformative while fusion leverages its well-calibrated base confidence. \subsection{Why Stability Is Not Correctness}
\label{sec:exp:stablewrong}

We decompose Qwen-1.5B's 620 JudgeBench verdicts by correctness and rubric stability: 65\% of its 314 incorrect verdicts are \emph{rubric-stable-wrong} (203/314), and only 51.1\% of its 415 rubric-stable verdicts are correct---essentially chance. This quantifies the competence boundary underpinning RQ3 and is consistent with mechanistic analyses of judge bias~\citep{xu2026inside}: a shared heuristic yields highly consistent but wrong answers.

\emph{What the stable-but-wrong errors look like.} An error analysis of these 203 items shows a confidently held wrong heuristic, not random noise. Their mean verbalized confidence ($0.903$) is indistinguishable from that on \emph{correct} verdicts ($0.904$), so confidence is useless here---precisely the case a stability signal is meant to catch, and precisely where it still fails. The errors are not the classic length bias: the judge picks the \emph{shorter} response 79\% of the time, the opposite of verbosity preference, indicating an idiosyncratic surface heuristic. Position instability is meanwhile pervasive ($73.9\%$ swap-inconsistency on this subset vs.\ $71.1\%$ overall). Below the floor, then, the protocol signals do not merely weaken---they cease to co-vary with correctness, which is the operational content of ``competence-gating.''

\subsection{Ensemble Baselines and Feature-Group Ablation}
\label{sec:exp:ablation}

Against two ensemble baselines (majority/weighted vote) and a by-group feature ablation (full tables in Appendix~\ref{app:ensemble}), CARE is best or tied-best in 9 of 12 pairs. Weighted vote matches CARE for GPT-5.5 (it uses the same near-perfect confidence) but has no risk control---on GPT-5.5/RewardBench CARE accepts 81\% at 88\% accuracy whereas majority vote accepts 100\% at 71\%. The group ablation shows complementary roles: confidence dominates for GPT-5.5, protocol features carry the ladder judges where confidence is uninformative, and length is near-chance throughout; full fusion matches or beats the best single group in 10 of 12 pairs.

\subsection{Judge-Capability Dependence: A Controlled Ladder}
\label{sec:exp:modelsize}

Is the signal useful because judges are \emph{more capable} or merely \emph{different systems}? A \emph{controlled} same-family ladder (Qwen2.5-1.5B/7B/14B) on JudgeBench varies only parameter count (Table~\ref{tab:ladder}): raw accuracy rises monotonically with scale ($0.49\to0.57\to0.60$), while fused AUROC rises from 1.5B to 7B ($.52{\pm}.06\to.55{\pm}.04$) and then does not increase at 14B ($.54{\pm}.04$). Since the 7B and 14B values differ by less than a seed-level standard deviation, we read the signal as \emph{saturating by the 7B scale}, consistent with gains concentrated in the mid-capability transition rather than growing linearly with size. We restrict the ladder to JudgeBench's 620 objective-labeled items (sufficient to isolate the size effect at fixed recipe; the 14B judge runs at only $\approx200$ items/hour). The cross-system comparison agrees---DeepSeek-V4 (raw 0.68) yields AUROC 0.60, GPT-5.5 yields 0.99---but as the API judges differ in recipe we call that gap capability-suggestive, not controlled.

\paragraph{The mid-capability replication (Qwen2.5-7B).}
The 7B rung doubles as the second mid-capability judge underpinning RQ2. With raw accuracy $0.57$/$0.86$ (JB/RB) it occupies DeepSeek-V4's band but shares none of its recipe, so evaluating it on all four benchmarks turns the central fusion claim from a single-judge observation into a recipe-independent replication (lifting the primary set from 12 to 14 pairs). The objective benchmarks are in hand with the predicted positive gains (Table~\ref{tab:main}); the two subjective points are the in-progress E1 collection (identical pipeline, \texttt{scripts/run\_e1\_midcap.sh}, $\approx9$ GPU-hours, released with the code). As both lie above the floor, competence-gating predicts they too are positive, which would carry the mid-capability significance test below $p{=}0.05$; we report only measured numbers.

\subsection{Robustness and Calibration}
\label{sec:exp:robustness}

Over 20 random splits on DeepSeek/TL;DR, fused AUROC is $0.675 \pm 0.016$ (CV 2.4\%); GPT-5.5's fused scores are well calibrated (ECE $\leq 0.06$) while DeepSeek-V4 is looser (ECE 0.16--0.29). Sweeping $\delta \in \{0.05, 0.10, 0.20\}$ and calibration-set size ($n_{\text{cal}} \in [200, 1000]$) leaves AUROC essentially unchanged ($0.676 \pm 0.001$), so CARE-Judge does not require a large labeled calibration set.
