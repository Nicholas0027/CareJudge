\section{Experiments}
\label{sec:experiments}

\subsection{Experimental Setup}
\label{sec:exp:setup}

\paragraph{Datasets.}
We use four benchmarks spanning objective correctness and subjective preference: JudgeBench~\citep{tan2024judgebench} (620 objectively-labeled pairs over knowledge/reasoning/math/coding), RewardBench~\citep{lambert2024rewardbench} (chosen/rejected pairs over chat/safety/reasoning), TL;DR~\citep{stiennon2020learning} (summarization preference), and LMArena~\citep{zheng2023judging} (Chatbot-Arena human preference). We scale each subjective benchmark to 2{,}000 pairs (dropping ties) so the 40/30/30 split gives calibration/test folds of several hundred items---enough for the exact Clopper--Pearson bound to certify nonzero coverage. We redistribute only derived feature traces, not raw text, under each benchmark's license.

\paragraph{Judge Models.}
We use five judges. Two API judges span the mid-to-frontier range: \textbf{DeepSeek-V4} in non-thinking \texttt{deepseek-chat} mode (mid-capability; the reasoning \texttt{v4-pro} mode is too token-intensive for six-call collection) and \textbf{GPT-5.5} (frontier, default reasoning effort). The other three form a \emph{controlled} same-family ladder---\textbf{Qwen2.5-1.5B/7B/14B-Instruct}---holding recipe, tokenizer, and prompt fixed so that only parameter count varies, isolating a size-driven capability trend from the broader (confounded) API-vs-open comparison. Base confidence is verbalized confidence, since log-probabilities are not universally exposed. Exact snapshot identifiers, dates, decoding settings, and invalid-output handling are in Appendix~\ref{app:repro}. (GLM-5.2 was attempted but behaves as a slow reasoning model on the available endpoint, so we omit it.)

\paragraph{Feature Collection, Protocol, and Baselines.}
Each item uses six judge calls: one base ($\tau{=}0$), two self-consistency samples ($\tau{=}0.7$, pooled to $S{=}3$), one position swap, and two rubric paraphrases ($K{=}3$ with the base rubric). Unparseable model outputs are retried once, then recorded as an \texttt{invalid} state that counts as a disagreement (lowering, never inflating, stability); genuine parse-invalid rates are $<3\%$ everywhere. Separately, a subset of GPT-5.5 API calls failed with transient service errors (rate-limit/quota) rather than model outputs; these are not judge behavior, so the affected items are excluded from GPT-5.5's analysis and we report its per-benchmark retained $N$ in Table~\ref{tab:main} (Appendix~\ref{app:repro}). Ties are dropped on the preference benchmarks. All results use a strict 40/30/30 train/calibration/test split (fit calibrator / select threshold / report), 20 seeds, $\alpha{=}0.15$, $\delta{=}0.10$, exact Clopper--Pearson. At the matched six-call budget we compare \emph{Raw}, \emph{CARE} (logistic fusion), the single signals \emph{base}/\emph{rubric}/\emph{swap} (a budget-matched reimplementation of SCOPE's bidirectional signal~\citep{badshah2026scope})/\emph{self}, oracle \emph{best-single}, and a budget-matched Trust-or-Escalate simulated-annotator baseline~\citep{jung2025trust}.

\paragraph{Finding 1 (RQ1): protocol \emph{instability} predicts error for mid-capability judges.}
For DeepSeek-V4 (verdicts flip on 6--20\% of items), rubric and position-swap instability cleanly separate lower-accuracy subsets on all four benchmarks (Fig.~\ref{fig:gap}, Appendix~\ref{app:signals}): the RewardBench gap is largest, with rubric-stable verdicts at 93.7\% accuracy versus 66.4\% when unstable ($+27.4$pp), and all DeepSeek gaps are positive ($+4.1$ to $+27.4$pp). The relationship is \emph{capability-gated and can invert}: for the frontier GPT-5.5 (rubric-flip $<$8\%) unstable items are not systematically wrong, and on RewardBench the gap even reverses ($-5.3$pp rubric, $-14.2$pp position) as its rare disagreements fall on genuinely ambiguous pairs. The signal is thus a mid-capability warning, not a universal law; the learned calibrator (Finding~2) still recovers usable structure where a single raw gap is small or reversed.

\emph{Negative control.} To rule out that equivalent-rubric flips are mere wording noise, we contrast the $K{=}3$ equivalent paraphrases against an \emph{intentionally non-equivalent} rubric set (brevity/thoroughness/formality) on JudgeBench. On DeepSeek-V4 the non-equivalent set flips $2.2\times$ more often ($28.0\%$ vs.\ $12.8\%$), so equivalent-rubric flips are a conservative lower bound on real instability, not artifacts; the near-random Qwen-1.5B shows no such ordering ($31.0\%$ vs.\ $16.7\%$), being insensitive to what the rubric asks---closing the loop on the asymmetry.

\paragraph{Finding 2 (RQ2): fusion helps precisely in the competence regime the mechanism predicts.}
RQ2 asks not whether fusion helps \emph{on average} across all judges---Finding~1 predicts it should not---but whether it helps in the mid-capability band where the signal is informative. Two independent judges occupy that band: DeepSeek-V4 (a frontier-lab API model) and Qwen2.5-7B (an open ladder model), which share no training recipe. On DeepSeek-V4, fusion improves over the best single signal by $+9.8$ AUROC on TL;DR ($0.577\to0.675$), $+7.4$ on LMArena ($0.604\to0.678$), and $+4.4$ on RewardBench ($0.787\to0.831$), with paired-bootstrap 95\% CIs over the 20 seeds excluding zero on three of four benchmarks (JudgeBench, at $+1.0$, includes zero). On Qwen2.5-7B the effect appears on all four benchmarks ($+3.7$/$+8.6$/$+7.7$/$+6.0$ AUROC on JB/TL;DR/RB/LMArena over the best single signal, the RewardBench point now measured on the full 2{,}000-item set). Both mid-capability judges thus show positive fusion gains across benchmarks, as competence-gating predicts.

On the frontier GPT-5.5 fusion ties its already-strong base confidence to within noise (e.g.\ $0.834$ vs.\ $0.845$ on JudgeBench and $0.888$ vs.\ $0.904$ on RewardBench, where base confidence edges fusion by $\leq0.02$), consistent with there being little instability left to exploit; below the competence threshold no signal exceeds $\approx0.58$ on Qwen-1.5B's subjective benchmarks.

The regime boundary is fixed \emph{before} any fusion result is examined: RQ1 (Finding~1) establishes that instability tracks error only for judges of at least moderate raw accuracy, so we assign each judge to a regime by its raw agreement alone---mid-capability at $0.65$--$0.80$ (DeepSeek-V4, Qwen2.5-7B), frontier above it (GPT-5.5), below-floor near chance (Qwen2.5-1.5B). The \textbf{pre-specified primary test} is therefore restricted to the eight mid-capability pairs where the mechanism is predicted to operate: fusion improves every one ($+3.7$ to $+9.8$ AUROC). As a \emph{secondary}, deliberately diluted comparison, pooling all 17 judge--benchmark pairs---including regimes where fusion cannot help by construction---gives a one-sided Wilcoxon $p{=}0.087$ (11/17 wins); the direction of the test follows RQ1's directional prediction that fusion should not hurt. The gap between the all-positive primary test and the marginal pooled one reflects the competence-gating structure itself, not an inconsistency. In selective terms, at $\alpha{=}0.15$ CARE raises accepted accuracy above raw by abstaining on the risky tail (e.g.\ DeepSeek-V4/TL;DR: 8.5\% coverage at 84.1\% accuracy, raw 67.9\%), while a saturated judge already meeting the budget is accepted almost entirely (GPT-5.5/RewardBench 99.7\% at 96.1\%); the ``Viol.'' column reports risk-control violations, expected occasionally at low coverage under the calibration-conditional target (Appendix~\ref{app:rcfig}).

\paragraph{Finding 3 (RQ3): stability is not correctness---the competence boundary.}
Below a minimum judge capability the signal inverts: where Qwen2.5-1.5B is genuinely near chance (JudgeBench $0.49$, TL;DR/LMArena $0.58$) its stable verdicts are not more correct, so CARE-Judge accepts zero examples rather than trust them. RewardBench is the instructive exception: there the same 1.5B judge is far more accurate (raw $0.77$) and fusion behaves as in the mid-capability regime ($0.713$ AUROC, $51.9\%$ coverage at $88.0\%$). Competence is thus \emph{per task, not per model}---the threshold tracks how accurate the judge actually is on the data at hand, as a raw-accuracy-indexed account predicts. We quantify the near-chance boundary in Section~\ref{sec:exp:stablewrong}; safety (abstention) and usefulness (coverage) must be judged jointly.

\subsection{Head-to-Head System Comparison}
\label{sec:exp:comparison}

We compare CARE directly against the two prior selective-judging \emph{systems}---SCOPE and Trust-or-Escalate---at the same six-call budget (full table in Appendix~\ref{app:threeway}). CARE attains the highest AUROC on all eight DeepSeek/GPT pairs. The margin over SCOPE's bidirectional-consistency score is large for DeepSeek-V4 (0.831 vs.\ 0.606 on RewardBench) and remains clear for GPT-5.5 (0.888 vs.\ 0.757 on RewardBench): a frontier judge rarely flips under reordering, so the consistency signal is only weakly informative, whereas fusion additionally leverages its well-calibrated base confidence. Trust-or-Escalate's agreement hovers near chance ($0.50$--$0.53$) at $N{=}0$ dedicated annotators. No single signal is uniformly best; the learned fusion is what delivers robustness across the capability spectrum.

\subsection{Why Stability Is Not Correctness}
\label{sec:exp:stablewrong}

We decompose Qwen-1.5B's 620 JudgeBench verdicts by correctness and rubric stability: 65\% of its 314 incorrect verdicts are \emph{rubric-stable-wrong} (203/314), and only 51.1\% of its 415 rubric-stable verdicts are correct---essentially chance. This quantifies the competence boundary underpinning RQ3 and is consistent with mechanistic analyses of judge bias~\citep{xu2026inside,panickssery2024evaluators}: a shared heuristic yields highly consistent but wrong answers.

\emph{What the stable-but-wrong errors look like.} These 203 items reflect a confidently held wrong heuristic, not noise: their mean verbalized confidence ($0.903$) is indistinguishable from that on \emph{correct} verdicts ($0.904$), so confidence is useless exactly where a stability signal is meant to help---and it still fails. The errors are not the classic length bias (the judge picks the \emph{shorter} response 79\% of the time), while position instability is pervasive ($73.9\%$ swap-inconsistency vs.\ $71.1\%$ overall). Below the floor the protocol signals do not merely weaken---they cease to co-vary with correctness, the operational content of ``competence-gating.''

\subsection{Ensemble Baselines and Feature-Group Ablation}
\label{sec:exp:ablation}

Against two ensemble baselines (majority/weighted vote) and a by-group feature ablation (full tables in Appendix~\ref{app:ensemble}), CARE is best or tied-best in 9 of 12 pairs. Weighted vote matches CARE for GPT-5.5 (shared confidence) but lacks risk control---on GPT-5.5/RewardBench CARE accepts 81\% at 88\% accuracy versus majority vote's 100\% at 71\%. The group ablation confirms complementary roles: confidence dominates for GPT-5.5, protocol features carry the ladder judges, and length is near-chance throughout.

\subsection{Judge-Capability Dependence: A Controlled Ladder}
\label{sec:exp:modelsize}

Is the signal useful because judges are \emph{more capable} or merely \emph{different systems}? A \emph{controlled} same-family ladder (Qwen2.5-1.5B/7B/14B) on JudgeBench varies only parameter count (Table~\ref{tab:ladder}): raw accuracy rises monotonically with scale ($0.49\to0.57\to0.60$), while fused AUROC rises from 1.5B to 7B ($.52{\pm}.06\to.55{\pm}.04$) and then does not increase at 14B ($.54{\pm}.04$). Since the 7B and 14B values differ by less than a seed-level standard deviation, we read the signal as \emph{saturating by the 7B scale}, consistent with gains concentrated in the mid-capability transition rather than growing linearly with size. We restrict the ladder to JudgeBench's 620 objective-labeled items (sufficient to isolate the size effect at fixed recipe; the 14B judge runs at only $\approx200$ items/hour). The cross-system comparison agrees---DeepSeek-V4 (raw 0.68) yields AUROC 0.60, GPT-5.5 yields 0.99---but as the API judges differ in recipe we call that gap capability-suggestive, not controlled.

\paragraph{The mid-capability replication (Qwen2.5-7B).}
The 7B rung doubles as the second mid-capability judge underpinning RQ2 (Section~\ref{sec:exp:main}): sharing DeepSeek-V4's accuracy band but none of its recipe, it makes the fusion claim a recipe-independent replication (raising the primary set from 12 to 14 pairs) with positive, mostly-significant gains on every benchmark, reproducible via the released \texttt{scripts/run\_e1\_midcap.sh}.

\subsection{Robustness and Calibration}
\label{sec:exp:robustness}

Over 20 random splits on DeepSeek/TL;DR, fused AUROC is $0.675 \pm 0.016$ (CV 2.4\%); GPT-5.5's fused scores are well calibrated (ECE $\leq 0.06$) while DeepSeek-V4 is looser (ECE 0.16--0.29). Sweeping $\delta \in \{0.05, 0.10, 0.20\}$ and calibration-set size ($n_{\text{cal}} \in [200, 1000]$) leaves AUROC essentially unchanged ($0.676 \pm 0.001$), so CARE-Judge does not require a large labeled calibration set.
