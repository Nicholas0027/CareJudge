\section{Experiments}
\label{sec:experiments}

\subsection{Experimental Setup}
\label{sec:exp:setup}

\paragraph{Datasets.}
We use four benchmarks spanning objective correctness and subjective preference: JudgeBench~\citep{tan2024judgebench} (620 objectively-labeled pairs over knowledge/reasoning/math/coding), RewardBench~\citep{lambert2024rewardbench} (chosen/rejected pairs over chat/safety/reasoning), TL;DR~\citep{stiennon2020learning} (summarization preference), and LMArena~\citep{zheng2023judging} (Chatbot-Arena human preference). We scale each subjective benchmark to 2{,}000 pairs (dropping ties) so the 40/30/30 split gives calibration/test folds of several hundred items---enough for the exact Clopper--Pearson bound to certify nonzero coverage. We redistribute only derived feature traces, not raw text, under each benchmark's license.

\paragraph{Judge Models.}
We use five judges. Throughout, ``below-threshold'', ``mid-accuracy'', and ``frontier'' refer to a judge's \emph{raw agreement on the benchmark at hand} (near chance, $0.65$--$0.80$, and above, respectively), not to the underlying model's general capability; the same model can fall in different bands on different benchmarks (Section~\ref{sec:exp:modelsize}). Two API judges cover the mid-to-frontier range as judges: \textbf{DeepSeek-V4} in non-thinking \texttt{deepseek-chat} mode (its judge agreement lands in the mid band; the reasoning \texttt{v4-pro} mode is too token-intensive for six-call collection) and \textbf{GPT-5.5} (frontier agreement, default reasoning effort). The other three form a \emph{controlled} same-family ladder---\textbf{Qwen2.5-1.5B/7B/14B-Instruct}---holding recipe, tokenizer, and prompt fixed so that only parameter count varies, isolating a size-driven trend in judge agreement from the broader (confounded) API-vs-open comparison. Base confidence is verbalized confidence, since log-probabilities are not universally exposed. Exact snapshot identifiers, dates, decoding settings, and invalid-output handling are in Appendix~\ref{app:repro}. (GLM-5.2 was attempted but behaves as a slow reasoning model on the available endpoint, so we omit it.)

\paragraph{Feature Collection, Protocol, and Baselines.}
Each item uses six judge calls: one base ($\tau{=}0$), two self-consistency samples ($\tau{=}0.7$, pooled to $S{=}3$), one position swap, and two rubric paraphrases ($K{=}3$ with the base rubric). Unparseable model outputs are retried once, then recorded as an \texttt{invalid} state that counts as a disagreement (lowering, never inflating, stability); genuine parse-invalid rates are $<3\%$ everywhere. Separately, a subset of GPT-5.5 API calls failed with transient service errors (rate-limit/quota) rather than model outputs; these are not judge behavior, so the affected items are excluded from GPT-5.5's analysis and we report its per-benchmark retained $N$ in Table~\ref{tab:main} (Appendix~\ref{app:repro}). Ties are dropped on the preference benchmarks. All results use a strict 40/30/30 train/calibration/test split (fit calibrator / select threshold / report), 20 seeds, $\alpha{=}0.15$, $\delta{=}0.10$, exact Clopper--Pearson. At the matched six-call budget we compare \emph{Raw}, \emph{CARE} (logistic fusion), the single signals \emph{base}/\emph{rubric}/\emph{swap} (a budget-matched reimplementation of SCOPE's bidirectional signal~\citep{badshah2026scope})/\emph{self}, oracle \emph{best-single}, and a budget-matched Trust-or-Escalate simulated-annotator baseline~\citep{jung2025trust}. Budget parity is enforced by scoring \emph{every} method from the \emph{same} six per-item calls: a single signal is a projection of that shared call set onto one view (the SCOPE-style swap score reads the base and swapped verdicts; self-consistency reads the two $\tau{=}0.7$ samples), not a method that issues fewer calls. A signal intrinsically needing fewer calls (SCOPE's core score uses only the two ordering calls) is not charged for the unused ones, so any AUROC gap reflects information content, not spend.

\paragraph{Protocol instability and judge error (RQ1).}
Across all four primary judges, rubric- and position-stable verdicts are more accurate than unstable ones on almost every benchmark (Fig.~\ref{fig:gap}, Appendix~\ref{app:signals}). For DeepSeek-V4 the rubric gap ranges from $+4.1$pp (JudgeBench) to $+27.4$pp (RewardBench). It persists for the frontier GPT-5.5 ($+27.0$pp on RewardBench) but its unstable subset is small (rubric-flip $<$8\%), bounding how much the signal can add once near-saturated (RQ2). The one systematic reversal is the below-threshold Qwen2.5-1.5B on RewardBench, where stable verdicts are \emph{less} accurate ($-10.9$/$-11.0$pp): a near-random judge is stably wrong, the mechanism we examine next.

\emph{Negative control.} To rule out that equivalent-rubric flips are wording noise, we contrast the $K{=}3$ equivalent paraphrases against an \emph{intentionally non-equivalent} rubric set (brevity/thoroughness/formality) on JudgeBench (two judges; full counts in Appendix~\ref{app:repro}). On DeepSeek-V4 the non-equivalent set flips $2.2\times$ more often ($28.0\%$ vs.\ $12.8\%$), so equivalent-rubric flips are a conservative lower bound on instability, not artifacts; the near-random Qwen-1.5B shows no such ordering, being insensitive to what the rubric asks. This single-dataset, two-judge control is suggestive rather than conclusive (Section~\ref{sec:conclusion}).

\paragraph{Where fusion helps (RQ2).}
RQ2 asks not whether fusion helps \emph{on average}---RQ1 predicts it should not---but whether it helps in the mid-accuracy band where the signal is informative. Two recipe-independent judges occupy that band: DeepSeek-V4 (a strong general model whose \emph{judge} agreement lands mid) and the open Qwen2.5-7B. On DeepSeek-V4 fusion beats the best single signal by up to $+6.3$ AUROC (LMArena, $0.615\to0.678$), with paired-bootstrap 95\% CIs over 20 seeds excluding zero on three of four benchmarks (JudgeBench, $+1.0$, includes zero). On Qwen2.5-7B it is positive on three benchmarks (up to $+7.6$ on RewardBench) but falls $3.0$ below the swap signal on JudgeBench, where a 7B open judge is near chance. Seven of the eight mid-accuracy pairs thus show positive gains, as competence-gating predicts.

On the frontier GPT-5.5, fusion ties its already-strong base confidence to within noise ($\leq0.02$ AUROC on JudgeBench and RewardBench), consistent with little instability left to exploit; below the threshold no signal exceeds $\approx0.58$ on Qwen-1.5B's subjective benchmarks.

The regime boundary is fixed \emph{before} any fusion result is examined: judges are assigned to a regime by raw agreement alone---mid-accuracy at $0.65$--$0.80$ (Qwen2.5-7B, DeepSeek-V4), frontier above it (GPT-5.5), below-floor near chance (Qwen2.5-1.5B). The pre-specified primary test restricts to the eight mid-accuracy pairs: fusion improves seven ($+1.0$ to $+7.6$ AUROC), the exception being Qwen2.5-7B on JudgeBench. Pooling all 17 pairs---including regimes where fusion cannot help by construction---dilutes this to a one-sided Wilcoxon $p{=}0.087$ (11/17 wins), exactly as competence-gating predicts. In selective terms, at $\alpha{=}0.15$ CARE raises accepted accuracy above raw by abstaining on the risky tail (e.g.\ DeepSeek-V4/TL;DR: 8.5\% coverage at 82.9\% accuracy, raw 67.9\%), while a saturated judge is accepted almost entirely (GPT-5.5/RewardBench 99.7\% at 96.1\%); ``Viol.'' reports risk-control violations, expected occasionally at low coverage (Appendix~\ref{app:rcfig}).

\paragraph{The competence boundary (RQ3).}
Below a minimum judge capability the signal inverts: where Qwen2.5-1.5B is near chance (JudgeBench $0.49$, TL;DR/LMArena $0.58$) its stable verdicts are not more correct, so CARE-Judge accepts zero examples rather than trust them. RewardBench is the exception: there the same 1.5B judge is more accurate (raw $0.77$) and fusion behaves as in the mid-accuracy regime ($0.713$ AUROC, $51.9\%$ coverage at $88.0\%$). Competence is therefore per task rather than per model---the threshold tracks how accurate the judge is on the data at hand. We quantify the near-chance boundary in Section~\ref{sec:exp:stablewrong}; safety (abstention) and usefulness (coverage) must be judged jointly.

\emph{Is the gate a deployed component or a post-hoc label?} We separate two senses of ``competence-gating.'' The three-band naming (below-threshold / mid-accuracy / frontier) is a post-hoc categorization for organizing the analysis, not an online detector. The \emph{operational} gate---accept vs.\ abstain---is, however, applied automatically: on exactly the pairs we would call below-threshold, no calibration-split confidence level meets the risk bound, so the threshold is $+\infty$ and coverage collapses to $0.000$ with no regime label supplied (Qwen2.5-1.5B on JB/TL;DR/LMArena; Qwen2.5-7B on the same three). The system abstains where the signal is uninformative by consequence of the risk criterion, not of our labeling. CARE still needs task-specific labels to fit and calibrate, and these do not transfer freely (Appendix~\ref{app:transfer}), so it is a per-task reliability layer. Versus a supervised reward model on the same labels, its value is abstention under a measured risk target with interpretable signals, plus the automatic zero-coverage collapse that flags an untrustworthy judge---which a plain reward model does not surface.

\subsection{Head-to-Head System Comparison}
\label{sec:exp:comparison}

We compare CARE against the two prior selective-judging \emph{systems}---SCOPE and Trust-or-Escalate---at the same six-call budget (full 17-pair table in Appendix~\ref{app:threeway}). CARE attains the highest AUROC on 14 of the 17 pairs, including all eight DeepSeek/GPT pairs; the three exceptions are Qwen judges on JudgeBench, where SCOPE's bidirectional score is marginally higher near the accuracy floor. The margin over SCOPE is large where position flips are rare (e.g.\ DeepSeek-V4 0.831 vs.\ 0.606 on RewardBench): a frontier judge rarely flips under reordering, so its consistency signal is weak, whereas fusion also leverages base confidence. Trust-or-Escalate hovers near chance ($0.50$--$0.53$) at $N{=}0$ dedicated annotators. No single signal is uniformly best; the learned fusion delivers robustness across the spectrum.

\subsection{Why Stability Is Not Correctness}
\label{sec:exp:stablewrong}

We decompose Qwen-1.5B's 620 JudgeBench verdicts: 65\% of its 314 errors are \emph{rubric-stable-wrong} (203/314), and only 51.1\% of its 415 rubric-stable verdicts are correct---essentially chance. This quantifies the competence boundary underpinning RQ3, consistent with mechanistic analyses of judge bias~\citep{xu2026inside,panickssery2024evaluators}: a shared heuristic yields consistent but wrong answers.

\emph{What the stable-but-wrong errors look like.} These 203 items reflect a confidently held wrong heuristic, not noise: their mean verbalized confidence ($0.797$) is indistinguishable from that on \emph{correct} verdicts ($0.785$), so confidence is useless exactly where a stability signal is meant to help---and it still fails. Such a gap between \emph{stated} and \emph{revealed} preference is well documented in choice modeling~\citep{yu2018quantum}, motivating our use of behavior under perturbation rather than self-reported confidence. Nor are they a length artifact ($55.2\%$ longer-response picks vs.\ $51.6\%$ overall; Appendix~\ref{app:length}), so the heuristic is semantic. Below the floor the protocol signals do not merely weaken---they cease to co-vary with correctness, the operational content of ``competence-gating.''

\subsection{Ensemble Baselines and Feature-Group Ablation}
\label{sec:exp:ablation}

Against two ensemble baselines (majority/weighted vote) and a by-group feature ablation (full tables in Appendix~\ref{app:ensemble}), CARE is best or tied-best in 12 of 16 pairs. Weighted vote is competitive for GPT-5.5 (it uses confidence implicitly) but has no abstention mechanism: a vote must rule on every item, whereas on DeepSeek-V4/TL;DR CARE abstains to 8.5\% coverage at 82.9\% accuracy versus the raw 67.9\%. The group ablation confirms complementary roles: confidence dominates for GPT-5.5, protocol features carry the ladder judges, and length is near-chance throughout.

\subsection{Judge-Capability Dependence: A Controlled Ladder}
\label{sec:exp:modelsize}

Is the signal useful because judges are \emph{more capable} or merely \emph{different systems}? A \emph{controlled} same-family ladder (Qwen2.5-1.5B/7B/14B) on JudgeBench varies only parameter count (Table~\ref{tab:ladder}): raw accuracy rises with scale ($0.49\to0.57\to0.60$) while fused AUROC rises from 1.5B to 7B ($.52\to.55$) then plateaus at 14B ($.54$, within a seed-level standard deviation of 7B), so the signal \emph{saturates by the 7B scale} rather than growing linearly with size. We restrict the ladder to JudgeBench's 620 objective-labeled items (the 14B judge runs at only $\approx200$ items/hour). Being a single-dataset, three-point same-family trend, it is suggestive rather than a full size$\times$recipe study (Section~\ref{sec:conclusion}); the cross-system comparison agrees but is confounded by recipe.

The 7B rung doubles as the second mid-accuracy judge underpinning RQ2: sharing DeepSeek-V4's accuracy band but none of its recipe, it makes the fusion claim a recipe-independent replication, positive on three of its four benchmarks.

\subsection{Robustness and Calibration}
\label{sec:exp:robustness}

Over 20 splits on DeepSeek/TL;DR fused AUROC is $0.675\pm0.016$ (CV 2.4\%); GPT-5.5 is well calibrated (ECE $\leq0.06$), DeepSeek-V4 looser (ECE 0.16--0.29). Sweeping $\delta\in\{0.05,0.10,0.20\}$ and $n_{\text{cal}}\in[200,1000]$ leaves AUROC unchanged ($0.676\pm0.001$), so CARE needs no large calibration set. Varying $\alpha$ from $0.05$ to $0.30$ traces the expected coverage/accuracy tradeoff (Table~\ref{tab:alpha}; risk--coverage curves in Appendix~\ref{app:rcfig}).
