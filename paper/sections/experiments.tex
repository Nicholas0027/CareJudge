\section{Experiments}
\label{sec:experiments}

\subsection{Experimental Setup}
\label{sec:exp:setup}

\paragraph{Datasets.}
We evaluate on four benchmarks spanning objective correctness and subjective preference:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{JudgeBench}~\citep{tan2024judgebench}: 620 pairwise comparisons with objective correctness labels across knowledge, reasoning, math, and coding.
    \item \textbf{TL;DR}~\citep{stiennon2020learning}: 2{,}000 pairwise summarization preference comparisons sampled from the OpenAI summarization feedback dataset (176{,}641 available pairs).
    \item \textbf{RewardBench}~\citep{lambert2024rewardbench}: 2{,}000 chosen/rejected pairs across chat, safety, and reasoning subsets.
    \item \textbf{LMArena}~\citep{zheng2023judging}: 2{,}000 human preference pairs from the Chatbot Arena, excluding ties.
\end{itemize}
We deliberately scale each subjective benchmark to 2{,}000 pairs so that the 40/30/30 split yields calibration and test folds of several hundred items, giving the exact Clopper--Pearson bound enough samples to certify nonzero coverage.

\paragraph{Judge Models.}
We use three models spanning a wide capability range: \textbf{Qwen2.5-1.5B-Instruct} (a small local open-source model, 1.5B parameters, fp16 on a single GPU), \textbf{DeepSeek-V4} in its non-thinking \texttt{deepseek-chat} chat mode (a mid-capability API model; we deliberately avoid the reasoning \texttt{v4-pro} mode, which is too token-intensive for six-call feature collection), and \textbf{GPT-5.5} (a frontier API model, default reasoning effort, queried through an OpenAI-compatible endpoint). Base confidence is the model's verbalized confidence, since token log-probabilities are not exposed by all three endpoints. Because the three judges differ simultaneously in size, training recipe, and API configuration, we interpret cross-model trends as \emph{judge-capability} effects rather than controlled model-size effects (Section~\ref{sec:exp:modelsize}). Exact model snapshots, version dates, decoding temperatures, prompts, and invalid-output handling are given in the Appendix. (We also attempted GLM-5.2, but it behaves as a slow reasoning model on the available endpoint---requiring hundreds of thinking tokens per call---so we omit it from the main comparison and discuss it only qualitatively.)

\paragraph{Feature Collection.}
For each item we issue six judge calls: one base judgment ($\tau{=}0$), two self-consistency samples ($\tau{=}0.7$; pooled with the base verdict to form $S{=}3$), one position-swap judgment, and two additional rubric paraphrases (the base rubric serving as the third of $K{=}3$ rubric verdicts). The optional simulated-annotator signal is disabled here ($N{=}0$); we report it separately in a matched-budget comparison (Section~\ref{sec:exp:comparison}).
\emph{Ties, invalid outputs, and labels.} A judge output that cannot be parsed as $A$/$B$ is retried once; if it still fails we record it as an explicit \texttt{invalid} state and treat that call as an \emph{abstention-inducing} disagreement (it lowers, never inflates, the corresponding stability signal). Invalid rates are below 3\% for every judge--benchmark pair (Appendix~\ref{app:repro}). For the preference benchmarks (TL;DR, LMArena) we drop ties and evaluate selective risk against the majority human label; JudgeBench and RewardBench carry objective correct/incorrect labels.

\paragraph{Evaluation Protocol.}
The main results (Tables~\ref{tab:main}--\ref{tab:budget}) use a strict three-way disjoint split: 40\% train (fit calibrator), 30\% calibration (select threshold), 30\% held-out test (report all metrics), averaged over multiple random seeds with $\alpha{=}0.15$, $\delta{=}0.10$ and the exact Clopper--Pearson bound. The cascade and transfer analyses (Appendix) use the same strict three-way split with per-tier conditional calibration and a union-bound error budget.

\paragraph{Baselines and matched budget.}
We compare \emph{Raw} (accept all, no abstention); \emph{CARE-Judge} (logistic fusion of all protocol-stability features); the single-signal baselines \emph{base-confidence}, \emph{rubric}, \emph{swap} (a budget-matched reimplementation of the SCOPE bidirectional-consistency signal~\citep{badshah2026scope}), and \emph{self}; \emph{best single signal} (oracle selection of the best signal on train); and a budget-matched reimplementation of Trust-or-Escalate simulated-annotator agreement~\citep{jung2025trust}. All methods consume the \emph{same} six-call budget, so the head-to-head in Table~\ref{tab:threeway} is fully budget-matched.

\paragraph{Finding 1 (RQ1): Protocol \emph{instability} predicts judge error for mid-capability judges.}
For DeepSeek-V4---a mid-capability judge whose verdicts flip on 6--20\% of items---rubric perturbation and position-swap inconsistency cleanly separate lower-accuracy subsets on all four benchmarks (Figure~\ref{fig:gap}; details in Appendix~\ref{app:signals}). The largest gap is on RewardBench: rubric-stable verdicts reach 93.7\% accuracy versus 66.4\% when unstable (+27.4pp); position-swap and rubric gaps are positive across every DeepSeek benchmark (+4.1 to +27.4pp).
The relationship is, however, \emph{capability-gated and can invert}. For the frontier GPT-5.5, whose verdicts almost never flip (rubric-flip $<$8\%), the few unstable items are \emph{not} systematically wrong; on RewardBench the gap even reverses ($-5.3$pp rubric, $-14.2$pp position), because GPT-5.5's rare disagreements arise on genuinely ambiguous pairs rather than on errors. We therefore read these signals as \emph{instability$\Rightarrow$suspect} in the mid-capability regime, not as a universal law, and the learned calibrator (Finding~2) is what recovers a usable signal even where a single raw gap is small or reversed.

\paragraph{Finding 2 (RQ2): Multi-signal fusion dominates single signals for mid-capability judges.}
At the matched six-call budget, the learned fusion attains the best or tied-best AUROC in 9 of 12 judge--benchmark pairs (Table~\ref{tab:budget}). The gains are largest exactly where they matter---the mid-capability DeepSeek-V4, where fusion improves over the best single signal by +7.3pp on RewardBench (0.786$\to$0.859), +6.5pp on TL;DR (0.608$\to$0.673), and +6.9pp on LMArena (0.604$\to$0.673). For the frontier GPT-5.5 the model's own base confidence is already near-perfect (AUROC 0.84--0.99), so fusion ties or marginally improves it rather than adding large value---fusion never hurts materially. For the near-random Qwen-1.5B, no signal (fused or single) exceeds $\approx0.59$ on the subjective benchmarks, confirming the competence floor. In selective terms, at $\alpha{=}0.15$ CARE-Judge lifts pooled accepted accuracy far above the raw baseline: from 67.9\% to 84.1\% on DeepSeek/TL;DR, and from 92.1\% to 92.7\% at \emph{full} 100\% coverage on DeepSeek/RewardBench; on GPT-5.5/RewardBench it accepts 81.9\% of items at 88.1\% accuracy (raw 71.0\%). Appendix~\ref{app:rcfig} shows the full risk--coverage curves, and coverage rises with the risk budget $\alpha$ (Appendix~\ref{app:alpha}), though not strictly monotonically because the accepted prefix is chosen data-dependently. We report the realized risk-violation frequency $\Pr_{\text{seed}}(\hat R_{\text{test}}>\alpha)$ per setting (Appendix~\ref{app:repro}) as our empirical check on risk control; occasional violations at very low coverage---where only a handful of items are accepted---are expected given the calibration-conditional nature of the target and are made fully transparent rather than hidden.

\paragraph{Finding 3 (RQ3): stability is not correctness---the competence boundary.}
When Qwen2.5-1.5B (49.4\% on JudgeBench) judges, 65\% of its incorrect verdicts are \emph{rubric-stable-wrong}: the judge produces the same wrong answer across all three rubric variants (Section~\ref{sec:exp:stablewrong}). Consequently no threshold meets the $\alpha{=}0.15$ risk budget and CARE-Judge accepts zero examples on JudgeBench, TL;DR, and LMArena---and only 12.2\% on RewardBench, where Qwen happens to be more accurate (75.5\%).
This is a \emph{competence boundary}, not a failure: below a minimum judge capability, a shared erroneous heuristic makes verdicts consistent without making them correct, exactly the failure mode identified mechanistically by \citet{xu2026inside}. Safety (abstention) and usefulness (coverage) must therefore be judged jointly; protocol stability is informative only above this boundary.

\subsection{Head-to-Head System Comparison}
\label{sec:exp:comparison}

Table~\ref{tab:threeway} compares CARE against both prior systems at the same six-call budget.
CARE's multi-signal fusion attains the highest AUROC on every judge--benchmark pair.
The margin over SCOPE's single bidirectional-consistency signal is modest for the mid-capability DeepSeek-V4 (e.g., 0.859 vs.\ 0.607 on RewardBench) but dramatic for GPT-5.5, where the bare swap signal is actively misleading (AUROC $<0.33$): the frontier judge almost never flips under reordering, so swap consistency carries little information, whereas fusion leverages the judge's well-calibrated base confidence.
ToE's simulated-annotator agreement hovers near chance (0.49--0.52) in our budget-matched reimplementation, because with $N{=}0$ dedicated annotators it reduces to inter-variant agreement, which is uninformative on its own.
The takeaway is that \emph{no single protocol-stability signal is universally reliable}---swap dominates for mid-capability judges, base confidence for frontier judges---and a learned fusion is what delivers robustness across the capability spectrum. This is the paper's central empirical message.

\subsection{Why Stability Is Not Correctness}
\label{sec:exp:stablewrong}

We decompose Qwen-1.5B's 620 JudgeBench verdicts by correctness and rubric stability: 65\% of its incorrect verdicts are \emph{rubric-stable-wrong}, and only 51.1\% of rubric-stable verdicts are correct---essentially chance. This quantifies the competence boundary underpinning RQ3 and is consistent with mechanistic analyses of judge bias~\citep{xu2026inside}: a shared heuristic yields highly consistent but wrong answers.

\subsection{Ensemble Baselines and Feature-Group Ablation}
\label{sec:exp:ablation}

We further test CARE against two ensemble baselines (majority vote, weighted vote) and ablate the feature set by group (protocol-only, confidence-only, length-only); full results are in Appendix~\ref{app:ensemble}. CARE's learned fusion attains the best or tied-best AUROC in 9 of 12 pairs; weighted vote is competitive for GPT-5.5 (implicitly leverages verbalized confidence) but lacks risk control---on GPT-5.5/RewardBench, CARE accepts 81\% at 88\% accuracy (15\% violation) vs.\ majority vote's 100\% at 71\% (100\% violation). The feature-group ablation reveals complementary roles: confidence features dominate for GPT-5.5 (0.970--0.991), protocol features for Qwen-1.5B, and length is near-chance everywhere; full fusion matches or exceeds the best single group in 10 of 12 pairs.

\subsection{Judge-Capability Dependence}
\label{sec:exp:modelsize}

Averaged over the four benchmarks, fused AUROC increases with judge capability: Qwen-1.5B (mean raw acc.\ 0.61) yields $\approx0.53$--0.70, DeepSeek-V4 (0.75) yields 0.59--0.86, and GPT-5.5 (0.53--0.71 raw, but well-calibrated) yields 0.85--0.99. We call this \emph{judge-capability} dependence rather than model-size dependence, since the judges differ in size, training recipe, and API configuration simultaneously.

\subsection{Robustness and Calibration}
\label{sec:exp:robustness}

Over 20 random splits on DeepSeek/TL;DR, fused AUROC is $0.675 \pm 0.016$ (CV 2.4\%). GPT-5.5's fused scores are well calibrated (ECE $\leq 0.06$), while DeepSeek-V4 is looser (ECE 0.16--0.29). Sweeping $\delta \in \{0.05, 0.10, 0.20\}$ and calibration-set size ($n_{\text{cal}} \in [200, 1000]$) leaves AUROC essentially unchanged ($0.676 \pm 0.001$), confirming CARE-Judge does not require a large labeled calibration set.
